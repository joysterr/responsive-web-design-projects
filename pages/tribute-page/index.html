Background

J. Robert Oppenheimer (1904‐1967) was an influential American theoretical physicist known for his early contributions to quantum mechanics. During World War II, he played a pivotal role as the scientific director of the Manhattan Project, overseeing the development of the atomic bomb. After the war, Oppenheimer became an advocate for international control of nuclear weapons but faced controversy and a security clearance revocation during the Red Scare. Despite his postwar contributions to academia and efforts toward arms control, his legacy is marked by the dual nature of his impact—both as a brilliant scientist and a central figure in the development of destructive nuclear weaponry.
